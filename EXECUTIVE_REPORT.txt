───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This project delivers a production-grade machine-learning pipeline that
predicts Urban Heat Island (UHI) intensity class — Low, Medium, or High —
at geolocated pixels across three climatically diverse cities: Santiago
(Chile, semi-arid Mediterranean), Rio de Janeiro (Brazil, humid tropical
coastal), and Freetown (Sierra Leone, tropical monsoon). The pipeline
combines Sentinel-2 satellite imagery (15 spectral bands), OpenStreetMap
building footprint geometry, and 11 derived geo-aware context features
into a 32-feature per-pixel representation. A soft-voting ensemble of
Random Forest, HistGradientBoosting, and Logistic Regression classifies
each pixel using the competition's own UHI Index formula (pixel
temperature divided by city mean, thresholds 0.98/1.02) applied to a
rank-based Kelvin-scale proxy, hybridized with Gaussian Mixture natural
breaks.

The headline achievement is on the Freetown scenario, where the model
trained on Santiago + Rio only and predicted Freetown without any
Freetown labels. It achieved F1 macro 0.689 on a spatial holdout with
a train-vs-test gap of 0.003 — essentially no overfitting. Santiago and
Rio, evaluated in a mixed-training regime, produced honest
cross-region F1 scores of 0.549 and 0.625 respectively against real
ground-truth labels. The pipeline demonstrates that per-city
normalization, spatial-block cross-validation, and physics-anchored
pseudo-labeling enable meaningful cross-climate transfer even with
only 2–3 training cities.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
2. PROBLEM STATEMENT
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Urban Heat Islands are localized zones where built-up surfaces,
reduced vegetation, anthropogenic heat, and diminished
evapotranspiration cause temperatures to exceed surrounding rural
areas. UHI intensity varies across neighborhoods and matters for
urban planning, public-health mapping, and climate adaptation.

The challenge: predict Low/Medium/High UHI class at ~14,000–21,000
geolocated pixels per city using only satellite imagery and building
footprint data, without access to direct thermal measurements. The
three target cities span three distinct climate zones, creating a
genuine out-of-distribution generalization problem. With only 2–3
training cities available, every cross-city prediction is an
extrapolation rather than interpolation.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
3. DATA SOURCES
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

• Sentinel-2 L2A (European Space Agency) — 15 spectral bands at 10 m
  resolution, obtained via Microsoft Planetary Computer STAC API.
  Median mosaics compiled per city from 15 cloud-filtered scenes.
• OpenStreetMap building footprints — per-city polygon shapefiles
  (Chile 191 MB, Brazil 151 MB, Sierra Leone 36 MB).
• UHI intensity labels — ~14k–21k per-pixel CSV entries per city,
  provided by the competition sponsor.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
5. FEATURE ENGINEERING (32–33 features across 5 tiers)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Tier 1 — Classical spectral indices (3 features)
  NDVI, NDBI, NDWI — vegetation, built-up, water signatures.

Tier 2 — Advanced spectral indices (8 features)
  SAVI, EVI (soil/atmospheric-corrected vegetation), BSI (bare soil),
  UI (urban), NDMI (moisture), NDVIre (red-edge canopy), MNDWI
  (enhanced water), water_vapor (B09 humidity proxy).

Tier 3 — Physical composite features (6 features)
  urban_heat = max(NDBI, BSI, UI)
  veg_cooling = max(NDVI, SAVI, EVI)
  water_cooling = max(NDWI, MNDWI)
  Plus three balance terms: veg_urban_balance, cool_heat_balance,
  veg_health (NDVIre minus NDVI).

Tier 4 — Building geometry (7 features, from OSM shapefiles within 100 m)
  building_count_100m, building_density_100m, building_coverage_ratio,
  building_mean_size, building_max_size, building_size_std,
  building_shape_complexity. Cached per-city to
  Data/cache/building_features_*.csv (skips 10-min recompute).

Tier 5 — Geo-aware context (11 features, the breakthrough tier)
  dist_to_center_km, dist_to_water_km, dist_to_forest_km,
  local_urban_frac_500m, local_veg_frac_500m, and one-hot land-use
  class (water, forest, dense_urban, open_field, bare, mixed). Derived
  from KMeans neighbor queries and NDVI/NDBI/NDWI/building-density
  thresholds.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
6. CROSS-CITY TRANSFER MECHANISMS (the core innovation)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

• Per-city z-score normalization: every spectral feature is z-scored
  within its own city. The model learns "hotter than this city's
  mean" rather than absolute spectral thresholds that collapse when
  climate changes.

• Spatial-block cross-validation: 60 KMeans clusters on lat/lon form
  training blocks. StratifiedGroupKFold ensures no two adjacent
  pixels end up in different folds. Defeats spatial autocorrelation.

• Per-fold SMOTE: synthetic minority oversampling runs inside each CV
  fold via imblearn Pipeline. No class-balancing leakage.

• Tight regularization: max_depth ∈ {6, 10}, min_samples_leaf ∈
  {25, 50}, C ∈ {0.001–0.5}. The model literally cannot memorize
  city-specific quirks; it has to find patterns that repeat.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
7. MODEL ARCHITECTURE
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Six models tuned independently with spatial-block GridSearchCV:
  • Random Forest (main)
  • HistGradientBoosting (main)
  • Logistic Regression (main)
  • SVM, KNN, Decision Tree (benchmarks, excluded from ensemble)

Final ensemble: soft vote of Random Forest + HistGradientBoosting +
Logistic Regression with weights [2, 2, 1]. Chosen for complementary
inductive biases (non-linear splits, sequential boosting, linear
direction) without the noise of overfitting benchmark models.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
8. TARGET-CITY ADAPTATION (without labels)
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

• Phase 1B — Covariate shift importance weighting: a classifier
  predicts train-vs-target; resulting weights tilt the final refit
  toward samples that look like target data.

• Phase 4A v2 — Physics-anchored stratified pseudo-labeling:
  - Compute physical heat score per target-city pixel
  - Rank pixels; take top 15% as pseudo-High, bottom 15% as
    pseudo-Low, middle 15% around median as pseudo-Medium
  - Sanity filter: keep only where model P(class) ≥ 0.20
  - Retrain with pseudo-labels at weight 0.3
  - 2 rounds; class balance forced regardless of model confidence

• Phase 4B — Neighbor voting: pixels with prediction entropy > 1.0
  get replaced with the majority class among their 10 nearest
  geographic neighbors.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
9. CLASSIFICATION STRATEGY — UHI INDEX FORMULA
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

• Compute heat_score = 0.9·model_ordinal + 0.1·physics_composite
• Compute Kelvin proxy via rank-based scaling to [293 K, 313 K]
• Compute UHI Index = pixel_K / city_mean_K
• Threshold strategies (averaged 50/50 as "hybrid_a50"):
    - Formula-calibrated: quantile cuts matched to learned proportions
    - GMM natural breaks: Gaussian Mixture 3-component cluster boundaries
• Safety Net 7: if final distribution falls outside [5%, 80%] per
  class, force a 1/3 equal split via heat_score ranking (never
  activates on healthy runs).

11. KEY INSIGHTS
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

(i) Per-city normalization is the single most important design choice
for cross-climate transfer. Absolute NDVI/NDBI thresholds break
across climates; relative ones (z-scored within city) generalize.

(ii) Spatial-block cross-validation is non-negotiable for geospatial
ML. Random splits leak spatial autocorrelation and produce
unrealistic CV scores that don't reflect true generalization.

(iii) Physics-anchored pseudo-labeling outperformed confidence-based
pseudo-labeling in our iterations. Model confidence on a held-out
target city is biased toward the majority class and produces
confirmation-bias collapse; physics rankings force class balance by
construction.

(iv) Geo-aware context features (distance to water, distance to
forest, local neighborhood density) added ~3–5 percentage points of
F1 over pure spectral features. Geographic reasoning matters.

(v) The "gap" metric requires context. Freetown's 0.003 gap vs.
Santiago's 0.181 gap looks like Santiago overfit — but Santiago's
gap is an expected artifact of including the target city in training
(70% of pixels are memorized, 30% are held out, giving a built-in
~0.15 gap). The Honest Generalization Eval cell reports held-out-only
F1 as the real number.

(vi) The Medium class is the hardest everywhere because it sits in a
narrow slice of the heat distribution. Low and High are easier to
separate; Medium is always where the confusion concentrates.

───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
12. LIMITATIONS
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

• Only 2–3 training cities — every cross-city prediction is
  extrapolation. More climatically diverse training cities would be
  the single biggest improvement (estimated +10–15 pp F1).

• No direct Land Surface Temperature (LST) data. All heat features
  are proxies. We attempted MODIS MOD11A1 integration via Planetary
  Computer but encountered coverage gaps over coastal Freetown and
  pivoted to a rank-based Kelvin proxy. Real LST integration would
  likely add +5–10 pp F1.

• OpenStreetMap coverage bias: Sierra Leone's OSM building shapefile
  is 5× smaller than Chile's. Informal settlements are
  under-represented, biasing model predictions for African cities
  toward "less urban." Google Open Buildings v3 would close this gap.

• Static imagery: one median mosaic per city; no seasonal or diurnal
  variance information. Multi-temporal feature engineering is
  possible but was out of scope.

• Medium-class imprecision: especially in Rio where the natural
  Medium band is narrow (18%), the model over-predicts Medium
  boundaries and precision drops below 0.30 even with honest F1
  above 0.62.

13. FUTURE WORK
───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

1. Integrate MODIS MOD11A1 LST or Sentinel-3 SLSTR LST for direct
   Kelvin temperature as an input feature. Expected +5–10 pp F1.
2. Train on 5–8 climatically diverse cities (add Lagos, Cairo,
   Phoenix, Helsinki, etc.) to move from extrapolation to
   interpolation. Expected +10–15 pp F1.
3. Replace OSM building footprints with Google Open Buildings v3 for
   better informal-settlement coverage, especially in African cities.
4. Add multi-temporal Sentinel-2 features (mean/std/range over 12
   months) to capture seasonal UHI dynamics.
5. Evaluate domain-adversarial training to make feature
   representations city-invariant explicitly.
